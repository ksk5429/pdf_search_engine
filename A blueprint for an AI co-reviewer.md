# ScholarPeer: A blueprint for an AI co-reviewer

**ScholarPeer is best built as a Claude-Agent-SDK–orchestrated, multi-agent retrieval-augmented system that fuses OpenScholar-style citation-grounded synthesis, MARG-style specialized reviewer agents, and ColPali visual retrieval over a MinerU-parsed corpus stored in Qdrant.** This architecture matters because the three dominant 2025–2026 benchmarks — ScholarQABench for literature synthesis, ViDoRe V3 for visual document retrieval, and OmniDocBench v1.6 for PDF parsing — now have clear state-of-the-art leaders whose outputs plug together cleanly. The backstory: MARG (D'Arcy et al., 2024) proved that multi-agent specialization roughly doubles the number of "good" reviewer comments over single-agent GPT-4, OpenScholar (Nature, Feb 2026) showed retrieval-grounded 8B models beat GPT-4o on citation accuracy, and MinerU 2.5-Pro (April 2026) achieved 95.69 on OmniDocBench v1.6 with a 1.2B model, surpassing models 200× larger. The implication is concrete: a small team can now assemble a research-grade peer-review assistant from mostly open components, with Claude Sonnet 4.6 or Opus 4.7 as the orchestrator, and expect citation fidelity on par with human experts rather than the 78–90% hallucination rate of ungrounded frontier models.

## Why a multi-agent architecture wins over one big prompt

The MARG paper established the foundational result: a single GPT-4 agent generating reviews produces generic comments over 60% of the time and only **1.7 "good" comments per paper**. MARG-S, which uses a leader agent coordinating worker agents (each fed a 4,096-token chunk) plus specialized expert agents for experiments, clarity, and impact, raises that to **3.7 good comments per paper — a 2.2× improvement** — and cuts generic comments to 29%. The mechanism is specialization plus context distribution: each expert sees tailored prompts (e.g., "design high-quality experiments given the main claims") and only the relevant paper chunks, which keeps working context clean.

MAMORX (NeurIPS 2024) extended this pattern with multimodal inputs (figures, citation graphs) and external knowledge retrieval, demonstrating that **reviews improve further when agents can fetch cited prior work rather than relying on parametric knowledge alone**. Paper Circle (arXiv 2604.06170, 2026) generalized this into a two-pipeline architecture — Discovery (retrieval + MMR diversity ranking) and Analysis (knowledge-graph construction with typed nodes for concepts, methods, experiments, figures) — which is the cleanest published template for ScholarPeer's shape. The takeaway for design: **do not try to build one giant reviewer agent**. Decompose into a leader, 3–5 specialized expert agents (novelty, methodology, clarity, reproducibility, related-work), and a final refinement pass.

## Retrieval stack: OpenScholar-style grounding with visual augmentation

OpenScholar sets the quality bar. Its 45M open-access paper datastore (~236M passage embeddings), combined with an iterative self-feedback inference loop, lets an 8B Llama-3.1 fine-tune **outperform GPT-4o by 5–6% on ScholarQABench correctness** while matching human-expert citation accuracy. Experts in the OpenScholar human study preferred its outputs over expert-written answers 51% of the time (8B) and 70% (GPT-4o-augmented), versus 32% for vanilla GPT-4o. Ai2 Scholar QA (arXiv 2504.10861) is the productionized descendant and should be studied as the reference implementation for attribution UI and retrieval orchestration.

For the corpus layer, **OpenAlex is the correct backbone**: 243M works versus Web of Science's 71M and Scopus's 65M, with 27.9–29.3% broader coverage than either proprietary database and 34,000+ indexed open-access journals. Semantic Scholar's API supplies citation graphs and TLDRs as a complement, and arXiv + PubMed Central fill STEM gaps. The 2025 Monash living-guidelines study confirmed OpenAlex had the best single-database coverage (98.6%) of any tested source. Use the "polite pool" (include `mailto=` in requests) for sub-100k daily call budgets.

For documents where figures, tables, and equations carry meaning — which is most scientific PDFs — text-only retrieval leaves points on the table. **ColPali and its successor ColQwen2.5-v0.2** encode page images directly via a vision-language backbone and a ColBERT-style late-interaction matcher, outperforming OCR→embed pipelines on ViDoRe V2/V3. ViDoRe V3 (released 2026 by ILLUIN + NVIDIA) is the current gold-standard benchmark with 26,000 pages and 3,099 queries across 6 languages. Qdrant 1.10+ supports multi-vector storage natively with binary quantization, making ColPali indexing practical; a public experiment showed binary quantization roughly **halves search latency** versus scalar quantization with negligible accuracy loss.

## PDF parsing is a solved problem if you pick the right tool

Document parsing was the historic bottleneck of scientific RAG. As of April 2026 it is not. **MinerU 2.5-Pro (1.2B params) scores 95.69 on OmniDocBench v1.6**, beating Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-5, and Qwen3-VL despite being two orders of magnitude smaller. The practical ranking for ScholarPeer:

| Use case | Tool | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Primary scientific PDF → Markdown | **MinerU 2.5-Pro** | SOTA accuracy, 0.21 s/page on an L4 GPU, handles formulas and tables |
| Bibliographic metadata + references | **GROBID** | Battle-tested (backs Semantic Scholar's S2ORC), fastest without GPU, best structured XML |
| Enterprise fallback (MIT license, no GPU) | **Docling** | IBM-backed, modular, permissive license, 0.49 s/page on GPU |
| Equation-heavy math papers | **Mathpix** or **MinerU** | Best inline/display formula extraction on the Dec 2025 benchmark |

A production pipeline should run GROBID for metadata/references (it remains significantly faster and more reliable than Docling for bibliography parsing per independent 2025 comparisons) and MinerU for full-text + figures + tables, then store outputs as both Markdown (for LLMs) and structured JSON (for the knowledge graph).

## When GraphRAG helps — and when it's overkill

Graph-RAG's value **scales with query complexity**, not corpus size. Microsoft's hierarchical community GraphRAG hits 86% accuracy versus 32% baseline on its enterprise benchmark, but costs roughly $33K to process a single 5 GB legal case due to LLM-heavy entity-relation extraction. For peer-review queries like "does this paper's novelty claim hold up against prior art?" — which require multi-hop reasoning across citations — graph structure pays off. For factoid retrieval ("what dataset did they use?"), vanilla dense+BM25 hybrid retrieval wins on latency and cost.

The 2025 consensus, synthesized across the GraphRAG-Bench, HippoRAG, and LightRAG evaluations, is that **the best Graph-RAG variant beats vanilla RAG by 5–20 points on multi-hop benchmarks but underperforms on time-sensitive and factoid queries**. LightRAG (HKU) offers the best cost-quality tradeoff for ScholarPeer's scale: roughly 6,000× token efficiency over MS-GraphRAG, ~$0.15 vs $4–7 per document indexed, and incremental graph updates without full rebuilds. HippoRAG's Personalized PageRank traversal is the strongest choice if multi-hop reasoning across cited works is the dominant query pattern. Recommendation: **start with hybrid dense+BM25+ColBERT late-interaction in Qdrant, add a lightweight LightRAG layer over entity-relation extractions from MinerU output, and only escalate to full MS-GraphRAG if evaluation shows multi-hop gaps**.

## Orchestration: Claude Agent SDK is the right default

The framework choice in 2026 is genuinely a toss-up among LangGraph, CrewAI, and the Claude Agent SDK, and the right answer depends on constraints. **LangGraph leads in production observability** (LangSmith integration, checkpointing, time-travel debugging) and has v1.0 stability as of October 2025. **CrewAI offers the fastest time-to-prototype** (~35 lines for a minimal multi-agent system) and powers 60% of Fortune 500 agent pilots. **AutoGen merged into Microsoft Agent Framework** in October 2025 for Q1 2026 GA, optimized for Azure/.NET shops.

For ScholarPeer specifically, the **Claude Agent SDK** is the strongest choice for three reasons. First, it ships the same tool-use loop and context management that powers Claude Code, which means file reads, web search, MCP servers, and subagents are first-class primitives rather than bolt-ons. Second, Anthropic's constitutional-AI safety layer is built in at the model level — important for a system that critiques others' research. Third, the `AgentDefinition` primitive lets you declare the MARG-style specialist agents (novelty expert, methodology expert, clarity expert) programmatically with per-agent tool lists, model tiers, and memory scopes, while still loading filesystem-based `CLAUDE.md` project conventions via `setting_sources=["project"]`. The trade-off is Claude-only model lock-in; if multi-provider is a hard requirement, LangGraph is the fallback.

A practical pattern: use **Opus 4.7 for the leader/planning agent** (benefits from deeper reasoning on review strategy), **Sonnet 4.6 for specialist agents** (cheaper and fast enough for section-level analysis), and **Haiku 4.5 for the refinement and formatting pass**. The SDK's `model: Literal["sonnet", "opus", "haiku", "inherit"]` parameter makes this routing trivial.

## The CLAUDE.md file is the most important configuration artifact

Evidence from Anthropic's own best-practices doc, HumanLayer's engineering blog, and the shanraisshan/claude-code-best-practice repo (20k+ GitHub stars) converges on a precise recipe. **Keep CLAUDE.md under 300 lines — HumanLayer's production file is under 60**. Claude Code injects it as a system reminder with the note "this context may or may not be relevant to your tasks," and will actively ignore bloated files whose contents don't match the current task. The forcing function is progressive disclosure: put only universally-applicable rules in the root CLAUDE.md and keep task-specific instructions in separate `docs/` markdown files, mentioned by path so Claude reads them only when relevant.

Content that belongs in CLAUDE.md for ScholarPeer: the retrieval API contracts (OpenAlex polite-pool email, Semantic Scholar rate limits), the fixed JSON schema for reviewer-comment outputs, the citation format convention (`[S2:paperId]` or `[OA:W1234]`), hard safety rules ("never fabricate a DOI; if retrieval fails, return null and log"), and the test command. Avoid negative-only constraints like "never use X" — always pair them with the intended alternative. Use `/init` to generate a starter, then prune aggressively. Complement CLAUDE.md with **hooks** for deterministic enforcement (e.g., a PostToolUse hook that validates every generated citation against the retrieval log) and **skills** for reusable workflows like "run the novelty-check subagent against Semantic Scholar."

## Putting it together: the reference architecture

The synthesis of these findings is a five-layer system. **Layer 1 (Ingest)** runs GROBID for metadata and MinerU 2.5-Pro for full-text, figures, and equations, producing paired Markdown + structured JSON. **Layer 2 (Index)** stores three vector types in Qdrant — dense sentence embeddings for semantic search, BM25 sparse vectors for keyword precision, and ColPali multi-vectors over rendered page images for figure/table retrieval — plus a LightRAG entity-relation graph built from MinerU's extracted concepts. **Layer 3 (Retrieve)** runs hybrid search (dense + BM25 in parallel via Qdrant's Query API, reranked by ColBERT/ColPali late interaction) against the local corpus, the OpenAlex API (243M works), and Semantic Scholar (citation graph). **Layer 4 (Reason)** is the Claude Agent SDK orchestration: an Opus-4.7 leader agent spawns Sonnet-4.6 specialist subagents (MARG-style: novelty, methodology, clarity, reproducibility, related-work), each with scoped tool access and a dedicated context window fed the relevant retrieved passages. **Layer 5 (Synthesize)** runs an OpenScholar-style self-feedback loop — generate draft, retrieve additional citations to fill gaps, refine — before a final Haiku-4.5 formatting pass produces the structured review with inline `[source:id]` citations.

Two critical guardrails make this production-grade. First, **every citation must be grounded in a retrieved passage** — enforce this with a PostToolUse hook that rejects any `[source:X]` not present in the session's retrieval log, echoing OpenScholar's approach to eliminating the 78–90% hallucination baseline. Second, **evaluate against ScholarQABench and a held-out set of real conference reviews** — the MARG paper's user-study protocol (16 PhD-level evaluators rating specificity, accuracy, helpfulness) is the gold-standard methodology and should be replicated at small scale before any public release.

## Where this leaves the field and what remains open

The striking lesson from the 2024–2026 literature is how fast the frontier has shifted. Eighteen months ago, citation hallucination was considered an intrinsic LLM failure mode; OpenScholar's Nature paper and its open-source release of code, weights, datastore, and index settled that — grounding works, and small open models beat frontier closed models when properly retrieval-augmented. Similarly, PDF parsing moved from a brittle multi-model pipeline problem (OCR + layout + table + formula models stitched together) to a single 1.2B VLM that beats GPT-5 on OmniDocBench. Visual retrieval went from research curiosity to a hard requirement for any corpus with figures, thanks to ColPali's ViDoRe dominance.

What remains genuinely unsolved for ScholarPeer-class systems: **multi-hop reasoning across citation chains** (HippoRAG and LightRAG help but neither matches a careful human reviewer tracing a claim back three papers), **calibrated uncertainty** (LLMs still confabulate confidence rather than expressing genuine epistemic state), and **long-context degradation during refinement loops** (the "context rot" phenomenon documented by multiple Claude Code practitioners means that each self-feedback iteration risks quality loss if context isn't aggressively pruned). These are the frontiers where a dissertation-scale ScholarPeer implementation can make a real contribution rather than just integrating existing parts — particularly if the target domain (such as structural health monitoring, where multimodal sensor-and-image fusion papers are proliferating) exposes failure modes that general-purpose benchmarks miss.

The bottom line: the infrastructure is ready, the architectural patterns are proven, and the remaining work is disciplined integration, rigorous evaluation, and domain-specific refinement — not fundamental research into whether any of this is possible.
